\begin{abstract}
A purely peer-to-peer intelligence network would allow any device to serve AI inference without relying on centralized providers. The cost of AI is rising---\$20/month subscriptions, \$200/month premium tiers, and per-token API charges that scale linearly with usage. Meanwhile, open-weight models with 3--14 billion parameters now match the quality of proprietary models on domain-specific tasks, and they run on consumer hardware. What is missing is a way to connect these small, capable models into a collective intelligence that exceeds any single node. We propose Samhati, a network where every device that uses AI also provides AI. Queries fan out to multiple independent nodes running specialized small language models. Nodes peer-rank each other's answers using BradleyTerry maximum likelihood estimation. Quality is enforced by competition, not by staking or compute verification. Cryptographic Proofs of Inference prevent replay and tampering. Results settle on Solana. The system requires no staking, no special hardware, and no permission to join.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The cost an individual spends on AI is growing rapidly. ChatGPT Plus costs \$20/month. Claude Pro costs \$20/month. OpenAI's Pro tier costs \$200/month. Google, Microsoft, and Perplexity each charge \$20/month for their AI products. A person using two or three of these services pays \$40--60 per month---\$500--700 per year---for access to intelligence that runs on someone else's hardware~\cite{openai-pricing}.

This is not a temporary state. OpenAI's CFO stated that ChatGPT Plus could rise to \$44/month~\cite{verge-pricing}. API costs for frontier models remain steep: GPT-4 Turbo charges \$10 per million input tokens and \$30 per million output tokens. As AI becomes essential---for work, education, medicine, code---the people who cannot afford these subscriptions are excluded from the most powerful technology of our generation.

The concentration of AI behind corporate APIs creates a deeper problem than cost. When three companies control the majority of AI inference, they become gatekeepers to intelligence itself. They can censor responses, throttle access, discontinue models, raise prices, or sell user data. Users have no way to verify what model served their request, whether their prompts are stored, or whether the quality they receive today will exist tomorrow. Humanity is becoming dependent on AI, and that dependency is being routed through a handful of private servers.

\subsection{Open Models Exist. Running Them Locally Does Not Scale.}

The open-source AI community has produced remarkable models. Meta's Llama~3 405B approaches GPT-4 quality on many benchmarks~\cite{llama3}. DeepSeek, Mistral, Qwen, and Gemma have released models competitive with proprietary alternatives. The technology to build intelligence is no longer secret.

But running a 70B or 405B parameter model requires hardware that no ordinary person owns. A Llama~3 405B model at FP16 needs approximately 810GB of VRAM---multiple A100 GPUs costing tens of thousands of dollars. Even a 70B model at Q4 quantization needs 35--40GB of RAM, exceeding most consumer devices.

The answer is not bigger models on bigger hardware. The answer is smaller, specialized models working together.

\subsection{The Case for Small Language Models}

Recent research demonstrates that small language models (1B--14B parameters) can match or exceed much larger models on domain-specific tasks:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item Microsoft Phi-2 (2.7B) matched Llama~2 70B on reasoning benchmarks~\cite{phi2}.
\item Microsoft Phi-3-mini (3.8B) competed with Mixtral 8x7B and GPT-3.5 on MMLU~\cite{phi3}.
\item Mistral 7B outperformed Llama~2 13B across all benchmarks at launch~\cite{mistral}.
\item Liquid AI's LFM-1B and LFM-3B outperformed models of similar and larger size using novel architectures based on continuous-time dynamical systems~\cite{liquid}.
\end{itemize}

The future of AI is not just bigger---it is specialized and hybrid~\cite{liquid}. A network of diverse small models, each strong in its domain (Code, Math, Reasoning, General), can collectively produce intelligence that rivals monolithic large models. This is the insight behind Samhati: a 3B Code specialist beating a 70B generalist on a coding question is not surprising---it is the expected outcome of specialization.

\subsection{Why Not Proof of Stake?}

Existing decentralized AI networks require nodes to stake tokens before participating~\cite{fortytwo}. This creates a barrier: you must buy tokens before you can contribute intelligence. The student in India with a laptop but no cryptocurrency is excluded. The researcher in Nigeria with a GPU but no exchange access cannot join.

Staking optimizes for capital, not quality. A well-funded node with a bad model earns the right to participate by spending money. Samhati optimizes for quality: the node with the best answer wins, regardless of how much money its operator has.

We replace economic proof (staking) with computational proof (Proof of Inference) and quality proof (peer ranking). The cost of attacking the network is compute---you must actually run inference---not capital.

\section{Swarm Inference}

A query enters the network and is classified by complexity: Easy, Medium, or Hard. Easy and Medium queries dispatch to 3 nodes. Hard queries dispatch to 5 nodes and include a debate round~\cite{debate}.

Each selected node runs inference independently using \texttt{llama.cpp} and returns an answer alongside a Proof of Inference. The fan-out is parallel---all nodes begin inference simultaneously.

The network does not require all nodes to run the same model. A round might include Qwen-7B, Llama-8B, and DeepSeek-Coder-7B answering the same question. Model diversity is a feature: it prevents single-model failure modes and produces a broader range of reasoning.

For hard queries, a debate round follows initial inference: each node sees all other answers and rewrites its own. Multi-agent debate improves accuracy by +12.8 percentage points on mathematical reasoning~\cite{debate}.

% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Peer Ranking}

After answers are collected, each pair is evaluated by a randomly assigned judge node. The judge is asked: \textit{``Which answer is better? Judge only on factual correctness, logical reasoning, and completeness.''}

Judge assignment is randomized per round using $\text{BLAKE3}(\text{timestamp} \| \text{prompt} \| N)$, preventing collusion. For $N$ nodes, $\binom{N}{2}$ pairs are each judged by exactly one randomly selected node. Judges cannot evaluate their own answers.

The ranking prompt explicitly instructs judges to ignore confidence, persuasive language, and style---only substance matters. This prevents the ``trust me, I'm right'' attack where nodes inject self-aggrandizing phrases.

% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{BradleyTerry Aggregation}

Pairwise preferences are aggregated using the BradleyTerry model~\cite{bt}, estimated via Majorization-Minimization~\cite{mm}.

Each node $i$ has a latent strength $\beta_i$. The probability that $i$ is preferred over $j$ is:

\begin{equation}
P(i \succ j) = \frac{e^{\beta_i}}{e^{\beta_i} + e^{\beta_j}}
\end{equation}

The MM algorithm iterates:

\begin{equation}
\beta_i^{(\text{new})} = \ln\!\left(\frac{\sum_j w_{ij} \cdot \mathbb{1}[i \succ j]}{\sum_j w_{ij} / (e^{\beta_i} + e^{\beta_j})}\right)
\end{equation}

where $w_{ij}$ is the ranker's ELO weight. Convergence requires $\max|\Delta\beta| < 10^{-6}$, typically in 10--25 iterations. Rankings from higher-ELO judges carry more weight.

% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{ELO Reputation}

Each node maintains an ELO rating~\cite{elo}, starting at 1500:

\begin{equation}
E(A \text{ vs } B) = \frac{1}{1 + 10^{(R_B - R_A)/400}}, \quad \Delta = K \times (\text{actual} - E)
\end{equation}

$K=32$ for nodes with fewer than 1000 rounds; $K=16$ for established nodes. ELO floor is 100. Failed proof verification triggers a 200-point slash.

Domain-specific ELO is tracked separately: a node rated 1800 for Code and 1400 for Math will be selected for coding queries but not mathematical ones. ELO determines both node selection (higher = more rounds) and ranking weight (higher = more influence in BradleyTerry).

% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Proof of Inference}

Each node produces a cryptographic proof from its output logprobs---the top-$K$ token probability distributions at each generation step. Tokens are grouped into 32-token chunks and BLAKE3-hashed from deterministic byte serialization.

The signable message is:

\begin{center}
\texttt{model\_hash $\|$ token\_count $\|$ chunk\_hashes $\|$ timestamp $\|$ node\_pubkey}
\end{center}

signed with Ed25519. The verifier checks: model hash, token count, chunk count, sequential indices, 5-minute freshness, registered \texttt{node\_pubkey}, and valid signature.

\textbf{What Proof of Inference is not.} It is not TOPLOC~\cite{toploc}---it does not hash internal layer activations or verify which model ran. It verifies that \textit{a} model produced specific output distributions, signed by a specific node, within a recent time window. Quality verification is handled by peer ranking (Section~3), not by the proof mechanism.

\textbf{Why not Proof of Stake?} Staking requires capital. A node operator must buy tokens before they can participate. This excludes the exact people an open intelligence network should serve. Proof of Inference requires compute---you must actually run a model---which is a physical cost that cannot be faked but does not require purchasing tokens from an exchange. The barrier is a laptop, not a wallet.

% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Network}

Nodes discover each other through Solana~\cite{solana} on-chain registration and iroh~\cite{iroh} QUIC gossip.

A single Ed25519 keypair derives the node's Solana address (base58), iroh NodeId (hex), and proof signing key. On startup, the node registers on Solana, reads all existing NodeAccount PDAs, connects via iroh QUIC with relay-assisted NAT traversal, and broadcasts its model and endpoint every 10 seconds.

New peers are verified against Solana registration before acceptance.

% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Incentive}

Nodes earn SMTI tokens per round. The winner receives 60\% of the base emission; the remaining 40\% is distributed by ELO weight. Domain specialists (Code, Math, Reasoning) earn a $1.5\times$ multiplier on matching queries.

On-chain demand counters track query domains in real time. Operators see what the network needs and respond: if 42\% of queries are Code, running a Code specialist earns more. The network self-balances through economic signal, not central planning.

There is no staking. Any device can join and begin earning immediately.

% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section{Settlement}

Each round settles on Solana via the \texttt{submit\_round} instruction. The on-chain record includes participant public keys, BLAKE3 proof hashes, signed ELO deltas, winner, domain tag, and emission amount.

\section{Security Analysis}

\textbf{Sybil attacks.} Fake nodes must run real inference (Proof of Inference requires valid logprobs). Low-quality answers lose ELO. The compute cost of maintaining many nodes exceeds the reward from gaming ranking.

\textbf{Collusion.} Judge assignment is derived from $\text{BLAKE3}(\text{timestamp} \| \text{prompt} \| N)$, unpredictable and changing every round. Colluders cannot control which pairs they judge.

\textbf{Replay.} Proofs expire after 5 minutes. Each proof is bound to a unique \texttt{node\_pubkey} via Ed25519.

\textbf{Model freeloading.} A node running a cheap 0.5B model will consistently lose to 7B models in peer ranking. Its ELO decays, reducing selection and rewards. The system self-corrects: quality wins, not model identity.

\textbf{Self-promotion.} Ranking prompts focus on factual correctness. Judges cannot evaluate their own answers. Persuasive language is explicitly excluded from evaluation criteria.

\section{Conclusion}

The cost of AI is rising. The best models are closed. Humanity is becoming dependent on intelligence controlled by a few corporations. Open-weight models exist but require hardware that individuals cannot afford. Small language models can match large ones on specialized tasks.

Samhati connects these small models into a collective intelligence. Every device that uses AI also provides it. Quality is enforced by competition---the best answer wins, regardless of the operator's capital. No staking, no special hardware, no permission required.

Open intelligence for everyone, forever.
